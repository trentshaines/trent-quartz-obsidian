## Hume’s Paradox (of Induction)
[[David Hume]] asserted that we cannot be certain about the beliefs arrived at about unobserved facts. We only tend to really believe in causal power because of familiarity, which has no place in knowledge. 

However, inductive logic still has its arguments. First, we must introduce a systematic discussion. 
- *demonstrative inference*
	- premise necessitate its conclusion
	- necessarily truth-preserving
	- classically analytic truths that sacrifice extension of content
- *nondemonstrative inference*
	- fails to be demonstrative
	- not necessarily truth-preserving
- *correct inductive inference*
	- inference that lends weight to support or make probable conclusions from premises
- *ampliative inferences
	- has a conclusion with content that is not present explicitly or implicitly in the premises
- *non-ampliative inferences*
	- has a conclusion that does not augment the content of the premises

It is clear that non-ampliative inferences are truth-preserving, but are the ampliative inferences that are truth-preserving? This is the same as the Kantian question: are there synthetic judgements a-priori?

- Making that premise synthetic a-priori is ridiculous, but maybe we can remedy some sort of a-priori truth. [[David Hume]] suggested that we could recourse inductive inference if we had some principle of uniformity of nature.

	- However, we never know enough to really satisfy a synthetic a priori truth, as for all we know, the future could change
	- We know that things have been largely uniform in the past, but to use induction as a justification for induction is circular.
	- Hume’s fork is between the two kinds of reasoning: 
		- Reasoning concerning relations of ideas (demonstrative, nonampliative)
		- Reasoning concerning matters of fact and existence (nondemonstrative, ampliative)
	- Hume’s problem of induction is regarding if the second category is empty. The issue is that we can’t have an ampliative inference if its deductive, as then the conclusion is true if the premises are, thus we would have truth-preservation and thus a demonstrative nonampliative truth. We also can’t justify an ampliative inference inductively, which would be circular. 
	- In summary: We cannot justify any kind of ampliative inference. If it could be justified deductively it would not be ampliative. It cannot be justified nondemonstratively because that would be circular. 
	- This is often called Hume’s Paradox, as it seems that the argument is heavily supported, although we know in our hearts that we have knowledge of unobserved fact.
## Attempted Solutions to Hume’s Paradox:
-  Direct attacks upon some of the arguments
	- Attempts to provide inductive arguments to support induction
	- Attempts to introduce a synthetic a priori principle of uniformity
- Inductive Justification
	- There have been claims that inductive can be supported inductively without using circularity. 
	- Max Black argues that the fallacy of circular argument involves assuming as a premise the conclusion to be proved. (petito principii which can also be called premise-circularity) However, self supporting inductive arguments do not have the conclusion appear as a premise:
		- Ex:
			- In most instances of the use of R2 in arguments with true premises examined in a wide variety of conditions, R2 has usually been successful, therefore in the next instance to be encountered of the use of R2 in an argument with true premises, R2 will be successful. 
			- Let R2 be: *Most instances of A’s examined in a wide variety of conditions have been B, therefore the next A to be encountered will probably be B*. 
			- Ask:
				- Basically, induction has be seen time and time again, inductively, to work?
	- Lewis Carroll showed that there is a distinction between premises and rules of inference. Any inference, inductive or deductive, must conform to some rule. But neither the rule nor any statement about the rule is to be incorporated into the inference as an additional premise. self-supporting arguments need to have three conditions. 
		1. The argument must have true premises
		2. The argument must conform to a certain rule
		3. The conclusion of that argument must say something about the success or reliability of that rule in unexamined instances of its application
		- Ex1: 
			- If snow is white, then modus ponens is valid
			- Snow is white
			- → Modus ponens is valid.
		- Ex2 (clearly ridiculous):
			- If affirming the consequent is valid, snow is white
			- Snow is white
			- → Affirming the consequent is valid
		- Ask:
			- so, we are defining induction by embedding it within a structure?
	- This can be referred to as rule-circularity, the argument exhibits a form whose validity is asserted by the very conclusion that is to be proved. 
	- We can easily create a rule-circular argument antithetical to the argument that says we have inductive ground to believe the opposite will happen in the future.
	- Black believes that we can only employ self-supporting argument to rules wee already know to be correct: one could go about rejecting inductive argument altogether, but inductive self-support simply bolsters existing arguments. 
	- Nelson Goodman: formalized this problem of induction being successful in the past, and said that it wasn’t really successful

- Scientific Inference as non-inductive:
	- In the modern scientific view, we establish theories not as generalizations of  observations, but as best theories such that our observed consequences follow. This is known as the *hypothetico-deductive method*. 
	- More formally, we begin with a general hypothesis and particular statements of initial conditions, and then a particular predictive statement is deduced. 
	- In the *hypothetico-deductive* method, deduction is used as a crucial part of the logic, but we are ultimately trying to verify the premise of deduction, the hypothesis. We must realize that in order to go from observed evidence to hypothesis, we must use induction. There are multiple potential hypotheses for the same evidence, so it is clearly inductive. Some have argued that the way we obtain the hypothesis does not matter. 
		- Some have put Hume at fault for not recognizing the strength of hypothetico-deductive method, but his arguments are extensible and sound.
- Deductivism
	- Karl Popper recognizes that *hypothetico-deductive* method is inductive, and believes induction has no place in science. He holds the psychological belief that there is uniformity in nature but recognizes it is not rational. 
	- Popper’s fundamental thesis is that falsifiability is the mark by which statements of empirical science are distinguished from metaphysical statements and from tautologies. 
	- Science never aims to confirm a hypothesis, but to set forth hypotheses that resist fallibility, and to also try to devise ways to falsify any existing hypotheses.
	- Basically, we only have *modus tollens* to work with. 
	- However, this leaves us with science as purely deductive, nonampliative, which means that the content of science is no greater than the content of our observations. 
	- Not all unfalsified hypotheses are on par - there are principles of selection among them. The greater falsifiability, the greater the hypothesis’s content. Tautologies lack empirical content because they do not exclude any possible state of affairs. Content varies inversely with probability - the possible states of affairs in which is it compatible. Whereas the *hypothetico-deductive* method asks for high probability hypotheses, popper asks for low probability hypotheses.
	- *corroboration*: a highly falsifiable hypotheses which is severely tested becomes highly corroborated. However, this concept is a nondemonstrative form of inference. 
- Synthetic a priori principles
	- Synthetic a priori principles can effectively substitute in for premises to render inference possible
	- Terminology
		- Formulas:
			- Satisfiable in that domain - some interpretation in that domain in which it becomes a true statement
			- Satisfiable - there is some nonempty domain such that the formula can be interpreted to be a true statement
			- Consistent - iff Satisfiable
			- Inconsistent - No true interpretation in any nonempty domain
			- Valid - Negation of inconsistent, true in every nonempty domain
			- Logically Contingent - truth value depends on domain, not necessarily true or false. Formally, a consistent formula whose negation is also consistent
			- When we interpret formulas, the domain plays a role analogous to the notion of a possible world. 
		- A priori
			- Known before sense data
		- A posteriori
			- Known after sense data
		- Self Contradictory
			- Statement whose falsity can be established solely by reference to the syntactic and semantic rules of the language.
		- Analytic
			- Logical truth that can be transformed into another by means of substituting definiens for definiendum. It’s truth can be established solely by reference to the syntactic and semantic rules of the language 
			- Can be thought to have no factual content, in the sense that it does not rule out any possible worlds (these statements are true in every possible rule, so we learn nothing about the domain)
		- Synthetic
			- Neither analytic nor self-contradictory - the truth value is indeterminate from pure syntactic rules. 
	- Descartes uses synthetic a priori - cogito

		- We still only know what is within the premise, how is it ampliative?
	- It’s difficult to really make a synthetic a priori statement. Take for example that anything colored is extended. If we were to see with long waves, we wouldn’t know anything really about the size of objects. It’s difficult to take 
	- According to Kant, geometry constitutes a necessary form into which our experience of objective physical reality must fit. 
	- Pure geometry is a priori, but not synthetic because all geometries are going to be logically sound.

	- A priori vs. innate

- Postulational Approach
	- In face of the suspicious synthetic a priori, some have sought to find a postulational foundation for scientific inference, meaning, what assumptions we must take to render science acceptable. 
	- Russell’s postulates
		- Quasi-Permanence:
			- Given any event A, it happens very frequently that, at any neighboring time, there is at some neighboring place an event very similar to A.
		- Separable causal lines:
			- It is frequently possible to form a series of events such that from one or two members of the series something can be inferred as to all the other members
		- Spatio-temporal continuity in causal lines:
			- When there is a causal connection between two events that are not contiguous, there must be intermediate links in the causal chain such that each is contiguous to the next (no action at a distance)
		- Structural 
			- When a a number of structurally similar complex events are ranged about a center in regions not widely separated, it is usually the case that all belong to causal lines having their origin in an event of the same structure at the center. 
				- Ask: seems fundamental to our psychology/ semantics. 
		- The postulate of analogy
			- Given two classes of events A and B, and given that, whenever both A and B can be observed, there is reason to believe that A causes B, then if, in a given case, A is observed, but there is no way of observing whether B occurs or not, it is probable that B occurs; and similar if B is observed, but the presence or absence of A cannot be observed. 
	- A presupposition of induction cannot rigorously be attributed by the presuppositions of science, as they rest upon induction. Instead, we should search for a minimal basis for a sufficient condition of induction, and Russel searches for this. He wants to fill in “Induction is an acceptable inferential too if and only if …”
- Probabilistic Justification
	- An inductive inference with true premises establishes its conclusions as probable. 
	- Issues: we must at least claim that inductive inferences with true premises often have true conclusions, and this claim is not immune to Hume’s skepticism.
	- Instead, we could identify probability with degree of rational belief. We could try to dissolve the problem into the fact that accepting inductive evidence is rational, but we know the real problem is that inductive evidence is circularly justified. Basically, we will not even know what evidence consists of because we need inductive methods, we don’t even know if we should accept more ‘probable’ conclusions. 
- Pragmatic Justification
	- Hans Reichenbach argues that Nature may be sufficiently uniform in suitable respects for us to make successful inductive inferences from the observes to the unobserved. Nature also may not. We know from Hume that we will not be able to tell which is the case. However, we are faced with a wager, and we should pragmatically choose to support induction. We either use the standard inductive method, or crystal gazing. If we are in the world where nature is uniform, we win, and if not, we didn’t lose. 
	- This argument isn’t convincing to me but it seems honest. We don’t know what ‘induction’ really means here. I like it because most of the other arguments seem to be implicitly using it but try to obfuscate them in order to justify something.
